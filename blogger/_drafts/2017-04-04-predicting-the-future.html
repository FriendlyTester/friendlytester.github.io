We need to try our best to avoid predicting the future when creating our automation architecture. I’ve seen too many teams who spend weeks/months building new architectures for the simplest of checks, because; ‘hey, we might want to do this one day…’ or ‘I reckon that could change in the future’. Guesses, shots in the dark; also, known as expensive and time consuming.

Predicting the future:

Hang on though Richard, I’ve had it where I successfully predicted the future, and the fact I’d added some features to my architecture saved me hours or days. Sure, me too, but they’re all too rare, and I’ve built far more features that have never been used, just sat their clogging up my architecture, sitting on the dream of one day.





Complexity:

‘Complexity emerges, and this OK!’. Through the journey of automating an application, at all layers, complexity will emerge, this is a positive thing. The application could become more complex, perhaps the UI changes to a more complicated framework. The automation architecture will get more complicated, especially as the checks you create become more complex. Being aware of this increase in complexity is a powerful skill. We should listen to it, and ask ourselves some questions like;

·      This is proving difficult to automate, is it really valuable to continue?
A difficult question to ask yourself, but an important one. We sometimes find ourselves trapped by our automation, fixated on the problems it’s presenting us, that we lose focus of the original problem. That original problem, was more than likely a testing one. Take yourself back to the conversation that started this work, now that you’ve learnt some more information about your architecture, perhaps it could be worthwhile re-discussing the original problem? The results of such a conversation my surprise you and perhaps automation isn’t the answer after all.

·      Is this product in an automatable state?
We can sometimes be too quick to jump into trying to automate something, without exploring the automatability of the product. Does the product have the right levels of testability to be automated? If something is difficult to test, you can be sure it’ll be harder to automate. So instead of adding conditions into your architecture to deal with such complexities, talk to the developers and see if we can increase the testability and in turn likely increase the automatability.

·      Where is the higher risk?

We often take our mind of risk when automating something, again similar to above we get so stuck into our code we can sometimes become blind to risk. I see two key risks when automating something. There is the risk that was identified in discussing the testing problem, and where it was deemed automation would be a good way to mitigate it. But if complexity shows it’s face during the creation of such automation, a second risk comes to the surface. That is the risk or breaking currently functioning automated checks or tools. It can be hard to identify these because we’re so fixated on automating the current, so what tends to happen is we proceed with the current task and deal with the mess that may arrive later. This poses several problems; we may lose coverage on some areas and the valuable information those checks were providing. Those checks were established and returning value, so we have no choice but to fix them now, which takes time, sometimes time you may not have. So where possible it’s important to keep an eye on existing checks, if you feel your changes are going to impact some, take moment to access the risks, and proceed accordingly.
